CHAPTER 21

The Business of Barbering

Owning and operating a successful barbershop

🎯 Why Study The Business of Barbering?

As you become more proficient in your craft and your ability to manage yourself and others, you may decide to become an independent booth renter or even a barbershop owner. In fact, most owners are barbers.

Even if you spend your entire career as an employee of someone else's barbershop, you should be familiar with the rules of business that affect the shop. It is also important to look at your career behind the chair as your own business.

To become a successful entrepreneur, you will need to attract employees and clients to your business and maintain their loyalty over long periods of time.

Key Point: Business knowledge will serve you well in managing your career and professional finances, as well as your business practices.

🏢 Types of Business Options

If you reach a point in your life when you feel that you are ready to become your own boss, you will have two main options to consider:

  • Owning your own barbershop
  • Renting a booth in an existing shop or salon

Business Ownership Structures:

Sole Proprietorship — A business owned by one person. The owner has complete control and is personally responsible for all debts and obligations.
Partnership — A business owned by two or more people who share responsibilities, profits, and liabilities. Partners pool their skills and talents.
Corporation — An ownership structure controlled by one or more stockholders. Incorporating protects personal assets and limits financial liability. Can be an S Corporation or LLC (Limited Liability Company).
Franchise — A form of business organization where a firm that is already successful (the franchisor) enters into a continuing contractual relationship with other businesses (franchisees) operating under the franchisor's trade name in exchange for a fee.

Franchise Considerations:

  • Franchisor does most of the marketing
  • Protected territories (another franchise cannot open within your area)
  • Franchise agreements vary widely in what you can and cannot do
  • Must pay franchise fee regardless of profitability
  • Always research the franchise and talk to other owners
  • Have an attorney read the contract

📋 Business Plan

A business plan is a written description of your business as you see it today and as you foresee it in the next 5 years (detailed by year). It is more of an agreement with yourself and is not legally binding.

Components of a Business Plan:

SectionDescription
Executive SummarySummarizes your plan and states your objectives
Vision StatementA long-term picture of what the business is to become
Mission StatementDescription of key strategic influences, market, services, quality
Organizational PlanOutlines employees and management levels, administrative structure
Marketing PlanResearch on target clients, their needs, wants, and habits
Financial DocumentsProjected financial statements, actual statements, analysis
Supporting DocumentsOwner's resume, personal financial information, legal contracts
Barbershop PoliciesPolicies ensuring all clients and employees are treated fairly

Business Plan Essentials:

  • General description of the business and services
  • Area demographics (population, age, income, education)
  • Expected salaries and cost of benefits
  • Operations plan with pricing structure
  • Expenses (equipment, supplies, repairs, advertising, taxes, insurance)
  • Projected income and overhead expenses for up to 5 years
  • A certified public accountant (CPA) can help gather accurate financial information

⚖️ Business Regulations and Laws

Business regulations and laws are any and all local, state, and federal regulations and laws that you must comply with when you decide to open your barbershop or rent a booth.

Key Areas to Comply With:

  • Contact local authorities regarding business licenses, permits, and regulations
  • Zoning and business inspections
  • Federal Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) guidelines
  • OSHA requires Safety Data Sheets (SDSs) for products
  • Federal laws regarding hiring and firing
  • Social Security and unemployment contributions
  • Workplace behavior regulations
  • Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) regulations
  • Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) accessibility requirements
Important: Ignorance of the law is no excuse for violating it. Always check with your regulatory agency if you have any questions.

The Importance of Record Keeping:

  • Good business operations require a simple and efficient record system
  • Proper business records are necessary to meet requirements of local, state, and federal laws
  • Records are of value only if they are correct, concise, and complete
  • Keep accurate record of all income and expenses
  • Retain all check stubs, cancelled checks, receipts, and invoices
  • A professional accountant or full-charge bookkeeper is recommended

🏪 Purchasing an Established Barbershop

Purchasing an existing barbershop could be an excellent opportunity, but you have to look at all sides of the picture.

Important Considerations:

  • Seek professional assistance from an accountant and business lawyer
  • You can purchase all assets or some/all of its stock
  • You are not purchasing the staff or clientele
  • No guarantee staff will be retained or clients will continue to return

What to Include in the Agreement:

  • Financial audit to determine actual value once current owner's bookings are removed
  • Written purchase and sale agreement
  • Complete and signed statement of inventory (goods, fixtures, etc.)
  • Investigation of any note, mortgage, lease, or bill of sale for defaults
  • Confirmed identity of the owner
  • Use of the barbershop's name and reputation for a definite period
  • Disclosure of all information regarding clientele and purchasing habits
  • Disclosure of facility conditions
  • Noncompete agreement stating seller will not work within specified distance
  • Employee agreement regarding staff staying with new ownership

🛡️ Protection Against Fire, Theft, and Lawsuits

As a business owner, you must protect the business, clients, and shop staff on several levels:

  • Ensure adequate locks, fire alarm system, burglar alarm system, and surveillance
  • Purchase liability, fire, malpractice, and burglary insurance
  • Do not allow policies to lapse while you are in business
  • Be thoroughly familiar with all laws governing barbering
  • Know safety and infection control codes
  • Keep accurate records of employees, salaries, lengths of employment, Social Security numbers

💺 Understand Booth Rental

Booth rental involves renting a booth or station in a barbershop or salon. This practice is popular in shops all over the United States.

Booth Renter Responsibilities:

  • Responsible for own clientele, supplies, record keeping, and accounting
  • Ability to be their own boss with very little capital investment
  • Keeping records for income tax purposes
  • Paying all taxes, including higher Social Security (double that of an employee)
  • Carrying adequate malpractice and health insurance
  • Complying with all IRS obligations for independent contractors
  • Using own telephone and booking systems
  • Collecting all service fees (cash or credit card)
  • Creating all professional materials (business cards, service menu)
  • Purchasing all supplies (back bar and retail)
  • Tracking and maintaining inventory
  • Budgeting for advertising and incentives
  • Paying for all continuing education
  • Adhering to state laws and regulations
Note: Some states do not recognize booth rental as an acceptable method of doing business. Check with your state regulatory agency.

👥 Personnel Management

Staffing Your Barbershop:

Personnel are your staff or employees. The size of your barbershop will determine the size of your staff. Large shops may require:

  • Receptionists
  • Barbers
  • Colorists
  • Manicurists
  • Assistants
  • Housekeepers

Managing Personnel:

  • Human resources (HR) is an entire specialty
  • Covers hiring, managing, and firing employees
  • Must be familiar with civil rights laws, EEOC, and ADA
  • Every business should have a written personnel policies and procedures manual
  • Every employee must read and sign it

When Interviewing Potential Employees:

  • Level of skill: Educational background, industry experience
  • Personal grooming: Do they look like professionals?
  • Image: Does their image reflect your business?
  • Overall attitude: Positive or negative? Self-motivated?
  • Communication skills: Can they understand and respond clearly?
  • Work history: Job stability? Bringing clientele?

📞 The Front Desk

The Reception Area:

  • First impressions count
  • Must be attractive, appealing, and comfortable
  • Barbershop's nerve center where retail merchandise is displayed
  • Phone system is centered here
  • Financial transactions are carried out here
  • Stock with business cards
  • Prominently display price list

The Receptionist:

  • Second in importance only to licensed professionals
  • First and last person the client contacts
  • Should have image that reflects your brand
  • Pleasant, patient, addresses each client by name
  • Efficient, friendly, consistent service
  • Thorough knowledge of all retail products

Booking Appointments:

  • Key duty of the receptionist
  • Must be done with care because services are sold in terms of time
  • Schedule to make most efficient use of everyone's time
  • Take down client's first and last name, phone number, email, service booked
  • Confirm appointment 1-2 days before

📢 Advertising and Marketing

Social Media:

  • Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, and Instagram are free to use
  • Great way to build awareness about your business
  • Engage your audience in an interactive format
  • Have the same username for all accounts
  • Get permission from clients if you use their image
  • Post regularly so followers pay attention

Advertising Methods:

  • Newspaper ads and coupons
  • Build a website
  • E-mail newsletters and discount offers
  • Direct mail to mailing lists
  • Giveaway promotional items
  • Window displays
  • Community outreach and volunteering
  • Client referrals

Advertising Budget:

As a general rule, an advertising budget should not exceed 3 percent of your gross income.

💼 Selling in the Barbershop

  • Upselling: Adding on additional services
  • Cross-promoting: Encouraging a client booked for a haircut to also get other services
  • Retailing: Selling take-home or maintenance products

Successful barbershop owners make their living by giving complete image advice every day.

✨ Elements of a Successful Barbershop

Planning the Barbershop's Layout:

  • Ample aisle space
  • Space for each piece of equipment
  • Quality mirrors
  • Restrooms for clients and employees
  • Handicap-accessible facilities
  • Good plumbing and lighting
  • Ventilation, air-conditioning, and heating
  • Sufficient electrical outlets
  • Storage and display areas
  • Attractive reception/waiting area

Building Your Business:

  • Develop a referral program
  • Create a loyalty program
  • Encourage staff to have guests prebook appointments
  • Follow up every visit to determine client satisfaction
  • Personally contact clients who haven't been in for more than 8 weeks

📝 Chapter 21 Key Takeaways

  • Business Structures: Sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation, franchise
  • Business Plan: Essential document with vision, mission, marketing, and financial plans
  • Legal Compliance: Business licenses, permits, OSHA, EEOC, ADA requirements
  • Financial Benchmarks: Typical net profit is 15% of gross income
  • Record Keeping: Essential for taxes, legal compliance, and business success
  • Booth Rental: Popular option with significant responsibilities
  • Personnel Management: HR policies, interviewing, payroll, benefits
  • Front Desk Operations: Reception area, receptionist duties, appointment booking
  • Marketing: Social media, advertising, referral programs
  • Client Retention: Prebooking, follow-up, loyalty programs
