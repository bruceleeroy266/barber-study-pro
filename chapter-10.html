CHAPTER 10

Properties and Disorders of the Hair and Scalp

Hair structure, growth cycles, disorders, and analysis techniques

🎯 Introduction to Trichology

Trichology (trik-OL-uh-jee) is the scientific study of hair, its disorders, and its care. The word comes from Greek trichos (hair) and ology (study of).

Barbers study hair properties to:

  • Understand how products and chemicals affect hair structure
  • Keep client's hair and scalp healthy
  • Differentiate between normal and abnormal conditions
  • Perform proper hair and scalp analysis before services

🏗️ Structure of the Hair

Hair is an appendage of the skin, composed chiefly of a fibrous protein called keratin (KER-uh-tin). Full-grown human hair is divided into two parts:

  • Hair Root — Portion enclosed within the follicle beneath the skin surface
  • Hair Shaft — Portion extending above the skin surface

Structures of the Hair Root:

  • Hair Follicle — Tubelike depression or pocket in the skin containing the hair root
  • Hair Bulb — Lowest part of the hair root; contains the dermal papilla
  • Dermal Papilla — Small, cone-shaped elevation at the base that supplies nourishment
  • Sebaceous Glands — Oil glands that secrete sebum to lubricate hair and skin
  • Arrector Pili Muscle — Small muscle that contracts causing "goose bumps"

Factors Affecting Hair Health:

  • Diet and nutrition
  • Blood circulation
  • Emotional disturbances
  • Endocrine glands
  • Medication

🔬 Layers of the Hair Shaft

The hair shaft has three layers:

  • Cuticle — Outermost layer of overlapping scales that protects inner structure. Healthy cuticle lies flat and gives hair shine.
  • Cortex — Middle layer making up 90% of hair weight. Contains melanin pigment and provides strength and elasticity.
  • Medulla — Innermost layer composed of round cells. May be absent in fine or naturally blond hair.
Key Point: The cortex is most important for chemical services because it contains pigment and structural bonds.

🎨 Hair Pigment

Natural hair color results from melanin pigment found within the cortex. There are two types:

  • Eumelanin (yoo-MEL-uh-nin) — Provides brown and black color to hair
  • Pheomelanin (fee-oh-MEL-uh-nin) — Provides natural hair colors ranging from red and ginger to yellow and light blond tones

All natural color depends on the ratio of eumelanin to pheomelanin. Gray hair contains only a few scattered melanin granules; white hair contains no melanin.

〰️ Wave Pattern

The wave pattern refers to the shape of the hair strand and is described as straight, wavy, curly, or extremely curly. Natural wave patterns result from genetics.

Wave Pattern and Cross Sections:

Wave PatternShape of Cross Section
Straight HairRound cross section
Wavy or Curly HairOval to round cross section
Extremely Curly HairElliptical cross section

Extremely Curly Hair Characteristics:

  • Grows in long twisted spirals
  • Cross sections appear flattened and vary in shape
  • Has a natural tendency to coil like a telephone cord
  • Usually has fine texture with individual strands winding together
  • Often has low elasticity, breaks easily, and tends to knot

🔄 Hair Growth Cycle

Average growth rate: about ½ inch per month. Hair growth occurs in cycles with three phases:

Three Phases of Hair Growth:

Anagen (AN-uh-jen) — The Growth Phase

New hair is produced. Cells are manufactured in the hair follicle faster than any other normal cell. About 90% of scalp hair is in this phase, lasting 3-5 years (up to 10 years).

Catagen (KAT-uh-jen) — The Transition Phase

Transition period between growth and resting. The follicle shrinks, hair bulb disappears. Lasts 1-2 weeks. Less than 1% of hair is in this phase.

Telogen (TEL-uh-jen) — The Resting Phase

Final phase where hair either sheds or remains until pushed out by new growth. Lasts 3-6 months. About 10% of scalp hair is in this phase.

Normal Hair Shedding:

It is normal to lose an average of 75-100 hairs per day. Eyebrow hairs and eyelashes are replaced every 4-5 months.

Hair Growth Facts:

  • Hair growth is NOT increased by shaving, trimming, cutting, singeing, or ointments
  • Scalp massage increases blood flow but has not been proven to increase hair growth
  • Only FDA-approved treatments proven to increase hair growth are minoxidil and finasteride

📍 Growth Patterns

Growth patterns result in creating hair streams, whorls, and cowlicks:

  • Hair Stream — Hair that flows in the same direction; result of follicles arranged uniformly
  • Whorl — Hair that grows in a circular or swirl pattern; most often seen at the crown
  • Cowlick — Tuft of hair that stands straight up; usually more noticeable at front hairline
Styles should be chosen to minimize the upright effects of cowlicks.

💇 Hair Loss (Alopecia)

Abnormal hair loss is called alopecia (al-oh-PEE-shah). Over 63 million men and women in the US suffer from abnormal hair loss.

Types of Abnormal Hair Loss:

Androgenic Alopecia — Hair loss from genetics, age, and hormonal changes. Known as male pattern baldness.
Alopecia Prematura — Early onset hair loss, frequently seen by age 40.
Alopecia Areata — Autoimmune disorder causing round patches of sudden hair loss. Can progress to total scalp hair loss.
Alopecia Totalis — Complete hair loss on the scalp.
Alopecia Universalis — Complete body hair loss.
Alopecia Senilis — Normal loss of scalp hair in old age.
Alopecia Syphilitica — Caused by syphilis; non-inflamed bald areas look moth-eaten.

FDA-Approved Hair Loss Treatments:

  • Minoxidil — Topical treatment applied twice daily; sold as Rogaine
  • Finasteride — Oral prescription for men only; sold as Propecia

Emotional Impact of Hair Loss:

Studies show bald men are perceived as:

  • Less physically attractive
  • Less assertive
  • Less successful
  • Less personally likeable
  • Older than actual age (average 5 years)

🔍 Hair Disorders (Noncontagious)

Canities (kuh-NISH-eez) — Technical term for gray hair. Congenital canities exists at birth (albinism); acquired canities develops with age.
Hypertrichosis (hy-pur-trih-KOH-sis) — Abnormal growth of hair in areas normally covered with vellus hair only.
Trichoptilosis (trik-op-tih-LOH-sis) — Technical term for split ends.
Trichorrhexis Nodosa — Technical term for knotted hair; characterized by brittleness and nodular swellings.
Monilethrix (mon-IL-ee-thriks) — Technical term for beaded hair; hair breaks easily between beads or nodes.
Fragilitas Crinium — Technical term for brittle hair; hairs may split at any point.
Ringed Hair — Variation of canities with alternating bands of gray and pigmented hair.

🦠 Scalp Disorders

Dandruff (Pityriasis):

Pityriasis (pit-ih-RY-uh-sis) is the technical term for dandruff — excessive production and accumulation of skin cells.

  • Pityriasis Capitis Simplex — Classic dandruff with scalp irritation, large flakes, itchy scalp
  • Pityriasis Steatoides — More severe form with greasy/waxy scales mixed with sebum; may cause seborrheic dermatitis
Current research confirms dandruff is caused by a fungus called malassezia (formerly pityrosporum), present on all human skin.

Fungal Infections (Tinea):

Tinea (TIN-ee-uh) is the medical term for ringworm. All forms are highly contagious.

  • Tinea Capitis — Ringworm of the scalp; red papules at follicle openings, hair becomes brittle
  • Tinea Barbae — Ringworm of the beard; inflamed patches with pustules
  • Tinea Favosa — Also called honeycomb ringworm; dry, sulfur-yellow cup-like crusts
Important: Clients with suspected or confirmed tinea must be referred to a physician and cannot receive services until cleared.

Bacterial Infections:

  • Folliculitis Barbae — Inflammation of hair follicles with inflamed pustules; commonly called barber's itch
  • Pseudofolliculitis Barbae — Also called razor bumps; chronic inflammatory condition resembling folliculitis
  • Sycosis Vulgaris — Chronic bacterial infection involving areas surrounding follicles
  • Furuncle — Boil; acute bacterial infection of hair follicle
  • Carbuncle — Result of acute, deep-seated bacterial infection; larger than furuncle

Parasitic Infestations:

  • Pediculosis Capitis — Head lice infestation. Lice feed on scalp blood causing severe itching. Highly contagious through contact with infected items.
  • Scabies — Contagious skin disease caused by mites that burrow under skin. Excessive itching leads to infections.
Critical: Clients with head lice or scabies must NOT be treated in the barbershop and should be referred to a physician immediately.

🔬 Hair and Scalp Analysis

Barbering services benefit from analyzing hair condition using four senses:

  • Sight — Observe whether hair looks dry or oily
  • Hearing — Listen to client information about hair, health problems, product experiences
  • Smell — Odor may indicate scalp disorders
  • Touch — Essential for analyzing hair condition and texture

Scalp Analysis Procedure:

  • Comb through hair gently while parting to check for abnormalities
  • Do NOT begin service if parasites are present
  • Do NOT proceed with chemical services if signs of irritation or abrasions exist
  • Refer clients with unusual pigmentation or growth to a physician

Hair Texture (Diameter):

  • Coarse — Largest diameter; strongest; more resistant to chemicals
  • Medium — Most common; considered normal standard
  • Fine — Smallest diameter; most fragile; processes faster

Hair Density:

Number of individual hair strands per square inch. Average: approximately 2,200 strands per square inch with about 100,000 individual strands on the head.

Hair ColorAverage Number of Hairs
Blond140,000
Brown110,000
Black108,000
Red80,000

Hair Porosity:

Ability of hair to absorb moisture. Related to the condition of the cuticle layer.

  • Low Porosity (Resistant) — Compact cuticle; naturally resistant to penetration; may require more alkaline solutions
  • Medium/Normal Porosity — Absorbs least amount of moisture; processes as expected
  • High Porosity (Overly Porous) — Absorbs quickly; usually result of overprocessing; dry, damaged, fragile

Porosity Test: Slide thumb and forefinger down dry hair strand toward scalp. Smooth = resistant; slight roughness = porous; very rough/breaks = overly porous.

Hair Elasticity:

Ability of hair to stretch and return to original length without breaking. Indicates strength of side bonds.

  • Normal: Stretches up to 50% when wet and returns
  • Curly/Wavy: May stretch up to 50% and return
  • Low: Breaks easily or fails to return — indicates damage

Elasticity Test: Gently tug wet hair strands. Normal elasticity stretches and returns without breaking.

📝 Chapter 10 Key Takeaways

  • Trichology is the study of hair and scalp
  • Hair has root (inside follicle) and shaft (visible) with three layers: cuticle, cortex, medulla
  • Hair made of keratin containing COHNS elements
  • Disulfide bonds are strongest; broken by chemical services
  • Two melanin types: eumelanin (brown/black) and pheomelanin (red/blond)
  • Hair grows in cycles: anagen, catagen, telogen
  • Normal shedding: 75-100 hairs per day
  • Alopecia = abnormal hair loss; many types exist
  • Only FDA-approved treatments: minoxidil and finasteride
  • Tinea (ringworm) is highly contagious — refer to physician
  • Pediculosis (lice) and scabies — do not service; refer immediately
  • Analyze texture, density, porosity, elasticity before services
  • Always use sight, hearing, smell, and touch for analysis
