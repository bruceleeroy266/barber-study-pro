## Question 1

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q1`  
**Section:** 2 — Why Women's Haircutting Matters  
**Learning Objective:** Recognize that a thorough consultation must precede every women's haircutting service.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-044`

### Question

Before beginning a women's haircutting service, what is the most important first step?

A. Select the cutting structure based on the client's inspiration photo  
B. Perform a thorough consultation to confirm goals, history, and expectations  
C. Drape the client and prepare the tools  
D. Choose the elevation angle that matches the desired shape

### Correct Answer

**B. Perform a thorough consultation to confirm goals, history, and expectations**

### Explanation

A thorough consultation is the foundation of every professional service. It confirms what the client wants, reveals hair history and lifestyle factors, and sets realistic expectations before any cutting begins. Choosing a structure or elevation without consulting the client risks a result that does not match the client's goals.

## Question 2

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q2`  
**Section:** 3 — Haircut Design Philosophy  
**Learning Objective:** Define the five design elements of haircutting.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-005`

### Question

In haircut design, **texture** refers to:

A. The direction the hair flows after cutting  
B. The surface quality of each strand and the overall head of hair  
C. The angle at which hair is held away from the head  
D. The overall silhouette of the finished cut

### Correct Answer

**B. The surface quality of each strand and the overall head of hair**

### Explanation

Texture describes the surface quality of the hair — whether it is fine, medium, or coarse. It directly affects how hair responds to tension, layering, and texturizing techniques. Movement, elevation, and shape are separate design elements.

## Question 3

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q3`  
**Section:** 3 — Haircut Design Philosophy  
**Learning Objective:** Explain how the five design elements interact when planning a haircut.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-005`, `fc-ch16-056`

### Question

A client has coarse, resistant hair and wants a precise bob with a solid bottom line. Which design element most directly affects the choice of structure and technique?

A. Movement, because coarse hair needs layers to move  
B. Texture, because coarse hair responds differently to tension and texturizing than fine hair  
C. Weight, because coarse hair always requires graduation  
D. Shape, because the silhouette determines the elevation angle

### Correct Answer

**B. Texture, because coarse hair responds differently to tension and texturizing than fine hair**

### Explanation

Texture influences how the hair behaves under tension and how it responds to cutting and texturizing. Coarse hair may need different section sizes, tension, and finishing techniques than fine hair. While shape, weight, and movement matter, texture is the element that most directly drives those practical choices.

## Question 4

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q4`  
**Section:** 4 — Four Foundational Haircuts Overview  
**Learning Objective:** Match each foundational cut to its primary elevation and weight effect.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-006`

### Question

Which foundational haircut uses little to no elevation and keeps maximum weight concentrated along the perimeter?

A. Graduated cut  
B. Uniform-layered cut  
C. Blunt cut  
D. Long-layered cut

### Correct Answer

**C. Blunt cut**

### Explanation

The blunt cut is defined by low or zero elevation. Keeping the hair close to its natural fall concentrates weight along the outside edge, creating a strong, solid perimeter. Graduated, uniform-layered, and long-layered cuts all use higher elevation to move or distribute weight.

## Question 5

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q5`  
**Section:** 4 — Four Foundational Haircuts Overview  
**Learning Objective:** Select the appropriate foundational structure for a client goal.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-007`, `fc-ch16-047`

### Question

A client wants a shoulder-length style with fullness and a soft, stacked shape through the back. Which foundational structure is most appropriate?

A. Blunt cut  
B. Graduated cut  
C. Uniform-layered cut  
D. Long-layered cut

### Correct Answer

**B. Graduated cut**

### Explanation

A graduated cut uses controlled elevation to build weight along a design line, creating the stacked or wedge-shaped silhouette the client wants. A blunt cut would create a solid perimeter without the stacked fullness, while layered cuts would remove weight rather than build it.

## Question 6

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q6`  
**Section:** 4 — Four Foundational Haircuts Overview / Section 5 — Blunt Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Distinguish between blunt and graduated weight placement.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-006`, `fc-ch16-007`

### Question

How does a graduated cut differ from a blunt cut in terms of weight placement?

A. A blunt cut builds weight gradually, while a graduated cut keeps weight at the perimeter.  
B. A graduated cut removes all weight, while a blunt cut leaves weight throughout.  
C. A blunt cut keeps maximum weight at the perimeter, while a graduated cut builds weight along a design line.  
D. Both cuts keep weight at the perimeter; only the cutting-line direction differs.

### Correct Answer

**C. A blunt cut keeps maximum weight at the perimeter, while a graduated cut builds weight along a design line.**

### Explanation

The defining difference between these two structures is how weight is placed. A blunt cut uses little to no elevation to keep weight at the bottom perimeter. A graduated cut uses controlled elevation to stack weight gradually along a design line, often at the nape or back of the head.

## Question 7

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q7`  
**Section:** 5 — Packet B1: The Blunt Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Define zero elevation and its effect on weight.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-012`

### Question

In a blunt cut, **zero elevation** means:

A. Hair is held straight out from the head at 90 degrees  
B. Hair is lifted overhead at 180 degrees  
C. Hair is not lifted away from the head before cutting  
D. Hair is overdirected away from its natural fall

### Correct Answer

**C. Hair is not lifted away from the head before cutting**

### Explanation

Zero elevation means the hair is combed to its natural falling position and cut without being lifted. This keeps the maximum weight at the perimeter, which is the goal of a blunt cut. Any elevation would begin to remove weight or create layers.

## Question 8

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q8`  
**Section:** 5 — Packet B1: The Blunt Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Apply correct head position and tension principles to a client scenario.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-014`, `fc-ch16-011`

### Question

A client asks for a chin-length blunt bob. During the cut, the client's head tilts forward and is not corrected. What is the most likely result when the head returns to its normal upright position?

A. The perimeter will be shorter in the back and longer in the front  
B. The perimeter will be uneven and no longer form a clean horizontal line  
C. The perimeter will have a strong diagonal-forward line  
D. The hair will be overdirected and lose its blunt weight line

### Correct Answer

**B. The perimeter will be uneven and no longer form a clean horizontal line**

### Explanation

Head position directly affects the cutting line. When the head tilts forward, the hair falls differently than it does in a neutral position. Cutting in that tilted state produces a line that looks straight at the moment but becomes uneven once the client sits upright.

## Question 9

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q9`  
**Section:** 5 — Packet B1: The Blunt Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Identify common blunt-cut mistakes from a scenario.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-015`, `fc-ch16-057`

### Question

A barber finishes a blunt cut and notices small dips and corners only after blow-drying the hair. Which mistake most likely caused this?

A. The barber used a round brush instead of a paddle brush  
B. The barber skipped cross-checking the line before drying  
C. The barber applied heat protectant before cutting  
D. The barber used a traveling guide instead of a stationary guide

### Correct Answer

**B. The barber skipped cross-checking the line before drying**

### Explanation

Cross-checking reveals dips, corners, and uneven weight that are not visible when following the original cutting line. Wet hair can also hide small errors that become obvious once the hair is dry and styled. The brush choice, heat protectant, and guide type are not the cause of an uneven blunt perimeter.

## Question 10

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q10`  
**Section:** 5 — Packet B1: The Blunt Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Compare the effect of horizontal and diagonal cutting-line directions.  
**Cognitive Level:** Analysis  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-021`, `fc-ch16-010`

### Question

In a blunt cut, how does changing from a horizontal cutting line to a diagonal-forward cutting line around the face affect the finished shape?

A. It creates stacked fullness at the nape  
B. It changes the shape around the face or perimeter rather than keeping a strong, even baseline  
C. It removes weight evenly throughout the haircut  
D. It preserves perimeter length while adding interior layers

### Correct Answer

**B. It changes the shape around the face or perimeter rather than keeping a strong, even baseline**

### Explanation

A horizontal cutting line produces a strong, even baseline. A diagonal-forward line changes the direction of the perimeter around the face or other areas, creating a different silhouette while still using low elevation. Stacked fullness, even weight removal, and interior layers are effects of other structures and techniques.
