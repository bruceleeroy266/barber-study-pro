CHAPTER 8

Basics of Electricity

Electrical safety, electrotherapy, and light therapy

🎯 Why Study Electricity?

Barbers use electrical tools daily — clippers, hair dryers, electric latherizers, high-frequency machines, facial steamers. Understanding electricity helps you:

  • Operate equipment safely and efficiently
  • Prevent electrical hazards (shocks, burns, fires)
  • Understand electrotherapy and light therapy
  • Comply with state regulations

⚡ Understanding Electricity

Electricity Movement of electrons along a conductor. A conductor carries electrical current (metals, human body). An insulator resists flow (rubber, plastic, glass).

Types of Current:

  • Direct Current (DC) — Flows in one direction; used in batteries and some specialized equipment
  • Alternating Current (AC) — Changes direction rapidly (60 times/second in U.S.); most barbershop equipment uses AC

📏 Measurements of Electricity

  • Ampere (amp/A) — Strength or rate of flow of electric current
  • Volt (V) — Pressure or force pushing current forward
  • Ohm (Ω) — Electrical resistance; higher resistance = less current
  • Watt (W) — Amount of electrical power being used (volts × amps)
  • Kilowatt (kW) — 1,000 watts; used for larger appliances
Household electricity is usually supplied at 110–120 volts AC. Tools are rated for specific voltage — never use on wrong voltage.

🛡️ Electrical Safety Devices

  • Circuit Breaker — Automatically interrupts circuit when too much current flows
  • Fuse — Metal strip that melts and breaks circuit if current is too high
  • GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter) — Detects imbalances and shuts off power to prevent shock (required near water)

Safety Rules:

  • Inspect cords and plugs regularly for damage
  • Never use tools with frayed cords or broken plugs
  • Keep electrical equipment away from water
  • Don't overload circuits
  • Unplug tools by pulling the plug, not the cord
  • Never attempt to repair electrical equipment yourself

⚡ Electrotherapy

Galvanic Current (DC):

  • Anode (positive/red) — Produces acidic reaction; used for firming and soothing
  • Cathode (negative/black) — Produces alkaline reaction; used for stimulating and softening
  • Anaphoresis — Forcing substances into tissues using negative pole
  • Cataphoresis — Forcing substances into tissues using positive pole

High-Frequency (Tesla) Current:

  • Alternating current producing heat, stimulating circulation, germicidal effects
  • Used with glass electrode for facial treatments
Contraindications for Electrotherapy: Pacemakers, metal implants, heart conditions, epilepsy, pregnancy, broken skin.

💡 Light Therapy

  • Infrared (IR) — Produces heat; relaxes muscles, increases circulation
  • Ultraviolet (UV) — Germicidal and anti-inflammatory effects; can damage skin with overexposure
  • Visible light — Different colors have different effects
Safety: Protect eyes with goggles. Avoid overexposure. Follow manufacturer guidelines.

📝 Chapter 8 Key Takeaways

  • AC is most common in homes/shops; DC used in some specialized devices
  • Key measurements: ampere (strength), volt (pressure), ohm (resistance), watt (power)
  • Safety devices: circuit breakers, fuses, GFCIs
  • Electrotherapy provides benefits but has important contraindications
  • Light therapy adds therapeutic effects; always use eye protection
  • Safety first — Never use damaged cords or overload circuits
