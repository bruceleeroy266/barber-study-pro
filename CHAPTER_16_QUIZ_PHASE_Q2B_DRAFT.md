## Question 11

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q11`  
**Section:** 6 — Packet B2: The Graduated Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Distinguish between traveling guide and stationary guide.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-017`, `fc-ch16-018`

### Question

In haircutting, what is the main difference between a traveling guide and a stationary guide?

A. A traveling guide stays fixed while a stationary guide moves with each section  
B. A traveling guide moves with each new section, while a stationary guide stays in one fixed position  
C. A traveling guide is used only for blunt cuts, while a stationary guide is used only for graduated cuts  
D. A traveling guide controls perimeter length, while a stationary guide controls interior layers

### Correct Answer

**B. A traveling guide moves with each new section, while a stationary guide stays in one fixed position**

### Explanation

A traveling guide moves with each new section, carrying the established length and angle forward through the haircut. A stationary guide stays in one place and is used to maintain a consistent perimeter or weight line in a specific area. Both can be used across different structures depending on the desired result.

## Question 12

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q12`  
**Section:** 6 — Packet B2: The Graduated Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Determine whether a graduated cut is appropriate for a client goal.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-047`, `fc-ch16-016`

### Question

A client wants a bob with fullness through the back but a softer overall shape than a one-length cut. Which structure best meets this goal?

A. Blunt cut  
B. Graduated cut  
C. Uniform-layered cut  
D. Long-layered cut

### Correct Answer

**B. Graduated cut**

### Explanation

A graduated cut uses controlled elevation to build weight along a design line, creating the stacked fullness the client wants in the back. It is softer overall than a blunt one-length cut because the weight is graduated rather than concentrated in a single heavy perimeter.

## Question 13

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q13`  
**Section:** 6 — Packet B2: The Graduated Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Explain why subsections must remain narrow and controlled in a graduated cut.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-059`, `fc-ch16-058`

### Question

Why should subsections remain narrow and controlled when cutting a graduated shape?

A. Narrow sections speed up the haircut by reducing the number of cuts needed  
B. Small sections make it easier to see the angle and maintain even elevation  
C. Narrow sections prevent the client from feeling the comb on the scalp  
D. Small sections allow the barber to skip cross-checking

### Correct Answer

**B. Small sections make it easier to see the angle and maintain even elevation**

### Explanation

Narrow subsections let the barber clearly see the cutting angle and maintain consistent elevation from one section to the next. Large sections hide errors and make it impossible to control the weight buildup, which leads to an uneven silhouette.

## Question 14

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q14`  
**Section:** 6 — Packet B2: The Graduated Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Predict how changing finger angle would alter the graduated silhouette.  
**Cognitive Level:** Analysis  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-022`, `fc-ch16-023`

### Question

A barber raises the finger angle while cutting a graduated section. What is the most likely effect on the finished silhouette?

A. The weight buildup will shift higher and the stack will become more pronounced  
B. The perimeter will become blunt and lose all graduation  
C. The hair will be cut at 90 degrees, creating uniform layers  
D. The sections will automatically become traveling guides

### Correct Answer

**A. The weight buildup will shift higher and the stack will become more pronounced**

### Explanation

Finger angle directly controls where weight is built and how the silhouette flows. Changing the finger angle changes the cutting angle, which shifts the weight line and alters the stack. A higher finger angle typically moves the weight buildup higher and changes the curve of the silhouette.

## Question 15

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q15`  
**Section:** 7 — Packet C1: The Uniform Layered Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Define 90-degree elevation and its relationship to uniform layers.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-025`, `fc-ch16-024`

### Question

A uniform-layered cut relies primarily on which elevation?

A. Little to no elevation  
B. 45-degree elevation  
C. 90-degree elevation  
D. 180-degree elevation

### Correct Answer

**C. 90-degree elevation**

### Explanation

A uniform-layered cut uses 90-degree elevation, holding the hair straight out from the head. This creates equal-length layers and distributes weight evenly throughout the head. Lower elevations keep weight at the perimeter, while 180-degree elevation is used for long layers.

## Question 16

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q16`  
**Section:** 7 — Packet C1: The Uniform Layered Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Recommend a uniform-layered cut for a client scenario.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-048`, `fc-ch16-060`

### Question

A client wants a lighter, more versatile haircut with soft movement and no heavy bottom line. Which structure is most appropriate?

A. Blunt cut  
B. Graduated cut  
C. Uniform-layered cut  
D. Long-layered cut

### Correct Answer

**C. Uniform-layered cut**

### Explanation

A uniform-layered cut distributes weight evenly throughout the head, creating movement and styling versatility. It removes the heavy bottom line of a blunt cut and avoids the stacked fullness of a graduated cut. A long-layered cut is better when preserving overall length is the priority.

## Question 17

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q17`  
**Section:** 8 — Packet C2: The Long Layered Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Define 180-degree elevation and perimeter preservation.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-028`, `fc-ch16-029`

### Question

In a long-layered cut, 180-degree elevation means the hair is:

A. Held straight out from the head  
B. Lifted overhead, parallel to the floor  
C. Combed to its natural fall without lifting  
D. Pulled forward toward the face before cutting

### Correct Answer

**B. Lifted overhead, parallel to the floor**

### Explanation

180-degree elevation lifts the hair straight up and overhead, parallel to the floor. This high elevation allows interior layers to be cut shorter while the perimeter falls to its original length. Lower elevations create different structures and weight placements.

## Question 18

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q18`  
**Section:** 8 — Packet C2: The Long Layered Cut  
**Learning Objective:** Define long layers and their primary effect.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-027`, `fc-ch16-030`

### Question

Which statement best describes a long-layered cut?

A. It removes weight evenly throughout using 90-degree elevation  
B. It preserves perimeter length while introducing shorter interior layers for movement  
C. It builds weight at the nape using controlled elevation  
D. It creates a strong, solid perimeter with little to no elevation

### Correct Answer

**B. It preserves perimeter length while introducing shorter interior layers for movement**

### Explanation

A long-layered cut keeps the outside edge long while removing interior weight with shorter layers. This creates movement and reduces bulk without sacrificing overall length. The other options describe uniform-layered, graduated, and blunt cuts.

## Question 19

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q19`  
**Section:** 9 — Packet D1: Hair Analysis: Texture, Density & Curly Hair  
**Learning Objective:** Define texture and density in hair analysis.  
**Cognitive Level:** Recall  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-031`, `fc-ch16-032`

### Question

In hair analysis, **texture** refers to strand diameter, while **density** refers to:

A. The direction hair grows from the scalp  
B. The total amount of hair on the head  
C. How much the hair shrinks when dry  
D. The curl pattern of the hair

### Correct Answer

**B. The total amount of hair on the head**

### Explanation

Texture describes the thickness of individual strands: fine, medium, or coarse. Density describes the total amount of hair on the head: thin, medium, or thick. Both must be assessed before choosing section sizes, tension, and cutting techniques.

## Question 20

**Question ID:** `CH16-Q20`  
**Section:** 9 — Packet D1: Hair Analysis: Texture, Density & Curly Hair  
**Learning Objective:** Manage a curly-haired client who brings a straight-hair inspiration photo.  
**Cognitive Level:** Application  
**Flashcard Reference:** `fc-ch16-051`, `fc-ch16-020`, `fc-ch16-019`

### Question

A curly-haired client brings a photo of a style created on straight hair. What is the most professional response?

A. Copy the photo exactly because the client knows what they want  
B. Explain how shrinkage will affect the result and adapt the design to the client's curl pattern and texture  
C. Refuse the service because the style is impossible on curly hair  
D. Cut the hair wet to match the photo length and let it shrink naturally

### Correct Answer

**B. Explain how shrinkage will affect the result and adapt the design to the client's curl pattern and texture**

### Explanation

Curly hair shrinks as it dries, so a straight-hair style cannot be copied exactly. The professional response is to analyze the curl pattern and texture, explain shrinkage honestly, and adapt the design to what will actually work for the client. Refusing the service or copying the photo without explanation would damage trust.
