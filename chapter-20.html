CHAPTER 20

Working Behind the Chair

Employment, compensation, and building your career

🎯 Why Study Working Behind the Chair?

Understanding what happens after graduation is crucial for your success. This chapter covers:

  • Transitioning from school to professional work
  • Different employment classifications and compensation structures
  • Financial management and budgeting
  • Building and maintaining a clientele
  • Selling products and services effectively
  • Professional development and career growth
Key Point: Success in barbering requires both technical skills and business acumen. Understanding employment structures and financial management is essential.

🎓 Moving from School to Work

What to Expect:

Making the transition from school to work can be challenging. While you may be thrilled to have a job, working for a paycheck brings duties and responsibilities you may not have considered.

Job Description:

A job description outlines all the duties and responsibilities of a particular position in a barbershop. When you receive one:

  • Read it thoroughly
  • Make notes and jot down questions
  • Ask your manager for clarification
  • Understand it is your responsibility to fulfill all functions listed

💼 Employment Classifications

Understanding your employment status is crucial for taxes, benefits, and legal responsibilities.

Employee Status
  • Work on salary, commission, or salary-plus-commission basis
  • Employer tells you when and where to work
  • Training may be provided or required
  • Employer withholds income and Medicare taxes
  • Employer provides Form W-2
  • May receive benefits (insurance, vacation)
  • Employer pays portion of Social Security tax
Independent Contractor Status
  • Rent a chair or work for percentage of proceeds
  • Must apply for tax identification number
  • Responsible for own taxes, licenses, and insurance
  • Shop owner provides Form 1099-MISC if you earn over $600/year
  • No benefits provided
  • Must have written agreement with shop owner
  • Sets own hours and schedule
Booth Renter Status
  • Actually setting up a small business within the shop
  • Requires business license, insurance, and tax ID
  • Lease space from shop owner
  • Solely responsible for own clientele, supplies, and bookkeeping
  • Handle all money transactions
  • Responsible for booking own appointments
  • Weekly or monthly rent obligation
  • Can become self-employed for relatively small investment

💰 Wage Structures

Types of Compensation:

Straight or Hourly Salary — Most often seen in chain or franchise shops. Provides chance to earn fixed income while building clientele.
Commission — Employer pays percentage of gross service sales you generate. Commission percentages vary greatly (40% to 70%). May take 2 years to build clientele for straight commission to be viable.
Salary-Plus-Commission (Guarantee) — Guarantees minimum base salary with percentage of amount over base. Often used to motivate employees.
Tips — Income in addition to regular compensation. Must be tracked and reported for income tax return. Tips of $20+ per month must be reported.

Factors Affecting Commission:

  • Level of experience
  • Number of clients the shop serves
  • Size of your clientele
  • Services performed
  • Retail sales

🏆 Thriving in a Service Profession

Habits of a Good Barbershop Team Player:

  • Put others first: Put the needs of the barbershop and client ahead of your own feelings or desires
  • Be true to your word: Choose words carefully and honestly; be someone who can be counted on
  • Be punctual: Scheduling is central to the business; being late inconveniences clients and coworkers
  • Be a problem solver: Recognize problems promptly and find ways to resolve them constructively
  • Be a lifelong learner: Continue to learn throughout your career; thinking you're done learning is limiting

Barbershop Teamwork:

Working in a barbershop requires practicing and perfecting people skills. A barbershop is very much a team environment.

Workplace Principles:

  • Strive to help: Be concerned with the success of others, not just yourself
  • Pitch in: Help with whatever needs to be done when not busy with clients
  • Share your knowledge: Be willing to share what you know and learn from coworkers
  • Remain positive: Resist giving in to maliciousness and gossip
  • Become a relationship builder: Build good working relationships with all team members
  • Be willing to resolve conflicts: Discuss conflicts with individuals, not with others in the shop
  • Be willing to be subordinate: Beginners start lower; work your way up
  • Be sincerely loyal: Loyalty is vital to the workings of a barbershop

📊 Employee Evaluation

Getting Feedback:

  • Ask for feedback from shop manager and key coworkers
  • Evaluations typically scheduled 90 days after hiring, then annually
  • Ask for help and feedback anytime you need it
  • Watch other barbers to identify who is really good
  • Focus on skills of successful barbers
  • Ask senior barbers to sit in on client consultations
  • Ask manager to observe your technical skills
  • Have trusted coworker watch and evaluate your retail selling

Finding a Role Model:

Model your behavior after someone having the kind of success you wish to have. Watch how they:

  • Treat their clients
  • Treat shop staff and manager
  • Book appointments
  • Formulate color or select products
  • Handle crises or conflict

💵 Manage Your Money

Financial Responsibility:

Many barbering professionals must research and plan for expenses on their own. Managing money is something everyone can learn to do.

Personal Budget:

A budget helps you:

  • Track where your money goes
  • Make sure you always have enough
  • Plan ahead and save for bigger expenses
  • Prepare for vacation, home, or business ownership

Repayment of Debts:

  • Responsible adults pay back debts (car loans, mortgages, student loans)
  • Not paying back loans is called defaulting
  • Defaulting can have serious consequences for personal and professional credit
  • Legal action can be taken if you fail to repay
  • Know precisely what you owe and what you earn
  • Understand payment terms, interest rates, and what you can afford

Reporting Your Income:

Important: Serious legal consequences for not reporting income include:
  • Fines and potential jail time
  • Decreasing your borrowing power
  • Reducing Social Security benefits for retirement
  • Lowering Bureau of Labor endorsement as sustainable industry

Best Practices for Tracking Income:

  • Keep a daily log of tips and additional income
  • Add total for the month on a single page
  • Report total cash income for taxes
  • Ethical barbers report income accurately

🛍️ Discover the Selling You

Upselling and Retailing:

Upselling Services — Recommending and selling additional services to clients (also called ticket upgrading).
Retailing — Recommending and selling products to clients for at-home use.

Principles of Selling:

  • Be familiar with features and benefits of products
  • Test all products yourself
  • Recommend only what the client really needs
  • Adapt your approach to meet needs and personality of each client
  • Some clients prefer soft sell; others comfortable with hard-sell
  • Self-confidence comes from knowing products and believing they're good
  • Generate interest by asking questions that determine needs
  • Never misrepresent services or products
  • Don't underestimate client's intelligence
  • Deliver sales talk in relaxed, friendly manner
  • Demonstrate product use when possible
  • Recognize right psychological moment to close sale
  • Once client offers to buy, quit selling

The Psychology of Selling:

People buy for different reasons:

  • Vanity: Want to look better
  • Personal satisfaction: Want to feel better about themselves
  • Problem-solving: Want to spend less time maintaining hair

Overcoming Objections:

  • Reword objection in way that addresses client's need
  • Acknowledge what client said
  • State things in terms of client's benefit
  • For price objections, acknowledge price and reiterate benefits
  • Offer free sample if possible

Retailing Tips:

  • Ask clients what products they use for home maintenance
  • Discuss products as you use them
  • Place product in client's hands when possible
  • Advise how recommended service provides personal benefit
  • Keep retail areas clean, well lit, and appealing
  • Inform clients of promotions and sales
  • Be informed about merits of professional vs. generic products
  • Offer quick styling lessons if time permits

👥 Building Your Clientele

Strategies for Building Business:

  • Word of mouth: Most effective; provide excellent service
  • Business cards: Always have them available
  • Social media: Post before-and-after photos (with permission)
  • Business card referrals: Give clients cards with their name on them
  • Local business referrals: Card swap with gyms, tailors, diners near barbershop
  • Public speaking: Available for speaking at local groups, PTA, organizations

Rebooking Clients:

The best time to get clients back in the shop is while they're still in your chair:

  • Talk about their next appointment before they leave
  • Ask questions and listen to answers
  • Talk about condition of their hair
  • Discuss hairstyling habits at home
  • Explain benefits of regular maintenance
  • Listen carefully to clues about what's happening in their lives

🌟 Keep Current Clients and Expand Your Client Base

Effective Techniques:

  • Send birthday cards: Ask for birthday info (month and day only), send card with special offer valid for that month
  • Provide consistently good service: Quality service must always be first concern
  • Be reliable: Always be courteous, thoughtful, and professional
  • Be respectful: Do not gossip or make fun of anyone
  • Be positive: Look for the positive in every situation
  • Be professional: Keep relationships appropriate; don't cross lines
  • Ask for email addresses: E-mail is now preferred mode of communication

Building Efficiency:

  • Be aware of how much time services take
  • Schedule accordingly
  • As you become more experienced, reduce time per service
  • Increased efficiency increases income

Giving Yourself a Raise:

  • Spend less money: Reduce spending to increase money left over
  • Work more hours: Choose times when shop is busiest
  • Increase service prices: After building loyal clientele and mastering services
  • Retail more: Most barbershops pay commission on product sales

Seek Professional Advice:

  • Research and interview financial planners
  • Speak to officers at local bank
  • Don't take anyone's advice without careful consideration
  • Be an informed consumer

🚀 On Your Way

Your first job in this industry will most likely be the most difficult. Getting started means spending time on a steep learning curve.

Keys to Success:

  • Be patient with yourself as you transition from "school you" to "professional you"
  • Remember practice makes perfect
  • You won't know everything you need to know right at the start
  • Be confident in your solid knowledge base from barbering school
  • Make use of generous and experienced professionals
  • Let them teach you the tricks of the trade
  • Commit to perfecting your technical and customer service skills
  • Always be willing to learn
  • Let the concepts in this book be your guide
  • Enjoy your life and reap the amazing benefits of a career in barbering
Remember: Success in barbering comes from combining technical excellence with business savvy, financial responsibility, and outstanding customer service.

📝 Chapter 20 Key Takeaways

  • Employment Classifications: Employee, Independent Contractor, Booth Renter — each with different tax and legal responsibilities
  • Compensation Types: Salary, commission, salary-plus-commission, tips
  • Teamwork: Put others first, be punctual, be a problem solver, remain positive
  • Financial Management: Create and stick to a personal budget
  • Debt Repayment: Defaulting on loans has serious consequences
  • Income Reporting: Report all income including tips of $20+ per month
  • Upselling: Recommending additional services to clients
  • Retailing: Selling products for at-home use
  • Client Building: Word of mouth, business cards, social media, referrals
  • Rebooking: Schedule next appointment while client is still in chair
  • Client Retention: Birthday cards, consistent service, reliability, respect
  • Increasing Income: Reduce spending, work more hours, raise prices, retail more
