# Chapter 4: Infection Control
## Study Guide Notes

---

### Publisher's Note – Clarifying Key Terms

Previous editions used sanitation/sanitizing interchangeably with "clean/cleaning." This edition clearly differentiates:

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Cleaning** | Mechanical process (scrubbing with soap/detergent + water) that removes visible dirt, debris, and many germs. **Required before disinfecting.** |
| **Sanitizing** | Chemical process that reduces germs on already-cleaned surfaces to a safe level |
| **Disinfecting** | Chemical process that destroys most harmful organisms (except bacterial spores) on nonporous surfaces and implements |

Professionals and scientific communities prefer consistent use of "cleaning" to avoid confusion.

---

### Introduction

State barber boards and regulatory agencies require:
- **Infection control measures**
- **Safe work practices**

**Infection control** minimizes spread of contagious diseases and skin infections (via person-to-person or contaminated tools/equipment).

**Safe work practices** prevent accidents and injuries.

As a barber, you are responsible for protecting client health and using safe practices to prevent workplace incidents.

---

### Why Study Infection Control?

- Know pathogens and their modes of transmission
- Safeguard your health, client health, and your business
- Comply with laws/rules and handle chemicals safely
- Daily precautions protect clients and your license
- Responsible barbers are conscientious about safety

### Federal Agencies

**LO1: Discuss federal and state agencies that regulate barbering.**

#### OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration)

- Part of U.S. Department of Labor
- Enforces workplace safety standards
- Created **Hazard Communication Standard (HCS)**
- Introduced **Globally Harmonized System (GHS)** with standardized labels and **Safety Data Sheets (SDS)** replacing MSDS

**SDS Features:**
- 16 standardized categories (identification, hazards, ingredients, first aid, firefighting, etc.)
- Uses **pictograms** for quick hazard recognition
- Must be kept available in the shop for all products
- Workers must read and sign off on them

#### EPA (Environmental Protection Agency)

- Registers all **disinfectants** used in the U.S.
- Disinfectants destroy most bacteria (except spores), fungi, and viruses on surfaces

**Two Main Types in Barbering:**

| Type | Use |
|------|-----|
| **Hospital disinfectants** | Effective against blood/body fluids on nonporous surfaces |
| **Tuberculocidal disinfectants** | Kill tuberculosis bacteria and other pathogens (required in some states when blood is present) |

**Key Disinfectant Types:**
- **Quaternary ammonium compounds (quats)** — common and effective
- **Phenolics** — strong, often tuberculocidal

**Caution:** Barbers cannot diagnose or treat infections/diseases — **refer clients to physicians**.

---

### Laws vs. Rules

| | Laws (Statutes) | Rules/Regulations |
|---|---|---|
| **Written by** | Legislatures | Agencies/boards |
| **Purpose** | Define scope of practice | More specific guidance |
| **Changes** | Rare | Can change frequently |

Barbers must know and comply with **both**.

### Principles of Infection

**LO2: List types and classifications of bacteria.**  
**LO3: Define bloodborne pathogens and explain transmission.**

Careless actions can cause injury or infection (invasion of body tissues by pathogens).

**Prevention starts and ends with you.**

**Four Harmful Organisms:**
1. Bacteria
2. Viruses
3. Fungi
4. Parasites

---

### Bacteria

- Single-celled organisms
- Some **nonpathogenic** (helpful), some **pathogenic** (harmful)

#### Classified by Shape:

| Shape | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Cocci** | Round | Staphylococci (grape clusters), Streptococci (chains) |
| **Bacilli** | Rod-shaped | Tetanus, TB |
| **Spirilla** | Spiral | Syphilis, Lyme disease |

#### Growth Stages:

- **Active stage** — Binary fission (rapid division)
- **Inactive stage** — Spores (dormant, resistant to disinfectants)

**Staphylococci (esp. MRSA)** — Common and dangerous; spread via skin contact or unclean tools.

**Signs of Infection:**
- **Inflammation** — redness, heat, pain, swelling
- **Pus** — sign of bacterial infection

---

### Viruses

- Submicroscopic
- Live inside host cells
- Examples: hepatitis, HIV, common cold, flu
- Many preventable by vaccines

---

### Bloodborne Pathogens

- Carried in blood/body fluids (hepatitis B/C, HIV)
- Spread in barbering when skin is broken (cuts during shaving/haircutting)

| Pathogen | Key Facts |
|----------|-----------|
| **Hepatitis B** | Easier to contract than HIV; present in all body fluids |
| **HIV/AIDS** | Weakens immune system; mainly blood-borne |

**Caution:** If blood is involved:
1. Stop service
2. Properly clean/disinfect tools
3. Follow exposure procedures

---

### Fungi

- Cause ringworm, tinea barbae (barber's itch), tinea capitis
- Clean/disinfect clippers thoroughly

---

### Parasites

- Head lice, scabies (itch mite)
- **Refer infested clients to a physician**
- Clean/disinfect tools with EPA disinfectant or bleach solution

---

### Immunity & Biofilms

- **Immunity** — natural or acquired resistance to pathogens
- **Biofilms** — protective microbial colonies; hard to remove

---

### Hand Washing & Sanitizers

Proper hand washing is one of the **most important infection control actions**.

- Removes germs from skin folds, nail grooves, and under free edge of nail
- Wash thoroughly **before and after each client**
- Use **liquid soap in pump containers** (bar soaps can harbor bacteria)
- Friction from soap bubbles helps remove pathogens
- Follow with **moisturizing lotion** (in pump container)
- Avoid very hot water

---

### Waterless Hand Sanitizers / Antiseptics

- Chemical germicides (high alcohol content) for **skin use only**
- Reduce microbes but **do not replace hand washing** when dirt is visible
- **Never use as surface or tool disinfectant**

#### Common Antiseptics in Barbering:

| Antiseptic | Use |
|------------|-----|
| **Hydrogen peroxide (3%)** | Good for skin but avoid open wounds |
| **Isopropyl alcohol** | Cleans skin but drying; **not a surface disinfectant** |

---

### Standard Precautions (LO6)

CDC guidelines assuming **all blood/body fluids are potentially infectious**.

- Treat every client as potentially carrying hepatitis B or other pathogens (many are asymptomatic)
- Includes: hand washing, gloves, proper handling of sharps, specific procedures for blood/body fluid exposure

#### Exposure Incident

**Definition:** Contact with non-intact skin, blood, or body fluids during duties.

**Immediate Actions:**
1. Stop service immediately
2. Follow detailed Procedure 4-4 for client and your safety

---

### Safe Work Practices and Safety Precautions (LO7)

Use common sense and observation to prevent accidents.

| Area | Precautions |
|------|-------------|
| **Water** | Careful with spray hose; test temperature; avoid scalding |
| **Electricity** | Keep appliances unplugged near water; use GFCI outlets; no overloading |
| **Tools & Appliances** | Strategic storage; disinfect before/after use; repair/replace damaged items |
| **Ventilation** | Proper air circulation to prevent inhalation of chemicals/dust |
| **Attire** | Comfortable, professional, non-restrictive; no dangling jewelry; supportive shoes |
| **Children** | Supervise closely; no playing on equipment |
| **Adult Clients** | Assist with chairs, support neck, use draping; be courteous and safety-conscious |

---

### Stropping, Leather Holsters & Multiuse Products

- **Leather (porous)** cannot be disinfected — clean and disinfect tools before/after contact
- Use **disposable neck strips/towels**; never let cape neckband touch skin
- **Multiuse containers** (creams, etc.) — use pumps, spatulas, or disposable tools to avoid contamination

**Caution:** Products without "disinfectant" on label are **cleaners only** — they must still be cleaned and disinfected after use.

---

### Professional Responsibilities (LO8)

Your responsibilities go far beyond cutting hair:

- **Never take shortcuts** on cleaning/disinfecting
- Follow all state/federal laws and rules
- Keep license current and updated
- Identify and eliminate hazards
- Be prepared for emergencies (contact lists, fire drills, SDS notebook)
- Maintain a knowledgeable, caring, professional manner

### KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. **Cleaning** = mechanical removal of dirt/germs; **Disinfecting** = chemical destruction of organisms
2. **OSHA** enforces workplace safety; **EPA** registers disinfectants
3. **SDS** (Safety Data Sheets) have 16 standardized categories and must be available in the shop
4. **Four harmful organisms:** Bacteria, Viruses, Fungi, Parasites
5. **Bacteria shapes:** Cocci (round), Bacilli (rod), Spirilla (spiral)
6. **Bloodborne pathogens:** Hepatitis B/C, HIV — spread through blood/body fluids
7. **MRSA** — dangerous staph infection spread via skin contact or unclean tools
8. **Hand washing** is the most important infection control action
9. **Standard Precautions** — treat all blood/body fluids as potentially infectious
10. **Leather cannot be disinfected** — it's porous
11. **Barbers cannot diagnose** — refer clients to physicians

### PRACTICE QUESTIONS

1. What is the difference between cleaning and disinfecting?
   → Cleaning removes visible dirt/germs mechanically; disinfecting destroys organisms chemically

2. What are the two main types of disinfectants used in barbering?
   → Hospital disinfectants and tuberculocidal disinfectants

3. What are the three shapes of bacteria?
   → Cocci (round), Bacilli (rod), Spirilla (spiral)

4. What are bloodborne pathogens?
   → Disease-causing organisms carried in blood/body fluids (hepatitis B/C, HIV)

5. What should you do if an exposure incident occurs?
   → Stop service immediately, follow Procedure 4-4

6. Why can't bar soap be used in barbershops?
   → Bar soaps can harbor bacteria; use liquid soap in pump containers

7. What does Standard Precautions mean?
   → Treat all blood/body fluids as potentially infectious

8. Can leather strops and holsters be disinfected?
   → No, leather is porous and cannot be disinfected
