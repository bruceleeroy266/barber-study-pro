# Chapter 9: The Skin – Structure, Disorders, and Diseases

### Introduction

The skin is the **largest organ of the body** and the body's **first line of defense** against disease and the environment. It protects internal organs and acts as a barrier.

**Dermatology:** Branch of medical science that studies the skin—its nature, structure, functions, diseases, and treatment.

**Dermatologist:** Physician specializing in skin, hair, and nails.

---

### Why Study the Skin – Structure, Disorders, and Diseases?

Barbers must have a thorough understanding because:
- Knowing skin composition and underlying structures is crucial for **safe facials, scalp treatments, and shaving services**
- You must **recognize normal skin conditions versus those requiring medical treatment**
- You must **identify abnormal skin conditions that prohibit certain services**

### Know the Anatomy of the Skin (LO1 & LO2)

Healthy skin is:
- Slightly moist
- Soft and flexible
- Smooth, fine-grained texture
- **Slightly acidic pH** (provides protective barrier against microorganisms)

**Skin renews itself** and varies in thickness:
- **Thinnest:** Eyelids
- **Thickest:** Palms and soles

**Calluses:** Form from continued pressure (natural defense — do not remove)

---

### Two Main Divisions of the Skin

#### 1. Epidermis (cuticle or scarf skin)

Outermost, thinnest protective layer with **no blood vessels**.

**Layers (strata) from deepest to surface:**

| Layer | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Stratum germinativum** (basal cell layer) | Deepest layer; responsible for growth; contains **melanocytes** (produce melanin pigment) |
| **Stratum spinosum** (spiny layer) | Sub-layer above basal; cells pushed upward |
| **Stratum granulosum** (granular layer) | Cells filled with keratin; almost dead |
| **Stratum lucidum** (clear layer) | Small, transparent cells; light passes through |
| **Stratum corneum** (horny layer) | Outermost; tightly packed, scale-like cells continually shed and replaced; waterproofed by sebum |

#### 2. Dermis (true skin, corium, cutis)

Underlying, thicker, highly sensitive layer (~25× thicker than epidermis).

**Papillary layer (superficial):**
- Contains papillae (conical projections)
- Tactile corpuscles (touch)
- Looped capillaries
- Some melanin

**Reticular layer (deeper):**
- Supplies oxygen/nutrients
- Contains: fat cells, sweat glands, blood vessels, hair follicles, lymph glands, arrector pili muscles, oil glands

#### 3. Subcutaneous Tissue (hypodermis or adipose tissue)

Layer of fatty tissue below dermis; provides:
- Smoothness
- Contour
- Energy storage
- Cushioning

---

### Melanin

- Pigment produced by **melanocytes** in basal layer
- Gives skin color (more in dark skin)
- **Protects against UV damage**

---

### Fluids of the Skin

**Blood and lymph** supply nourishment (protein, carbohydrates, fat).

About **half the body's blood supply** goes to the skin.

---

### Nerves of the Skin

| Nerve Type | Function |
|------------|----------|
| **Motor nerve fibers** | Control arrector pili muscles (goose bumps) |
| **Sensory nerve fibers** | React to heat, cold, touch, pressure, pain |
| **Secretory nerve fibers** | Regulate sweat and oil glands |

**Sense of Touch:** Papillary layer houses receptors for touch, pressure, pain, heat, cold.

---

### Skin Characteristics

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Skin Color** | Determined by genetics and melanin; darker skin has more melanin |
| **Skin Elasticity** | Provided by **collagen** (strength/support) and **elastin** (elasticity); healthy skin expands and returns to shape |

---

### The Glands of the Skin

#### 1. Sudoriferous (Sweat) Glands

- Coiled base + duct to skin surface (pore)
- **Regulate body temperature** by evaporating sweat
- Eliminates waste: salt, chemicals
- More numerous on palms, soles, forehead, armpits
- Influenced by heat, exercise, emotion, drugs

#### 2. Sebaceous (Oil) Glands

- Connected to hair follicles
- Secrete **sebum** (oily substance) to lubricate skin/hair and prevent moisture loss
- Most active on face and scalp
- **Blocked sebum + debris forms:**
  - **Blackhead** (open comedo)
  - **Whitehead** (closed comedo)

---

### Absorption Level of the Skin

- Limited beyond top layer
- Used for topical medications
- Depends on skin thickness and product concentration

---

### Functions of the Skin (SHAPES Mnemonic)

| Letter | Function |
|--------|----------|
| **S** | **S**ensation (touch, pressure, pain, heat, cold) |
| **H** | **H**eat regulation (maintains ~98.6°F body temperature via blood flow and sweat) |
| **A** | **A**bsorption (limited; some medications/creams) |
| **P** | **P**rotection (barrier against pathogens, injury, chemicals; waterproof via sebum) |
| **E** | **E**xcretion (perspiration removes waste) |
| **S** | **S**ecretion (sebum lubricates skin/hair) |

**Did You Know?** Many drugs are more effective when absorbed through the skin.

### Identify Disorders and Diseases of the Skin (LO3)

**Lesion:** Mark on skin indicating injury/damage or disease.

**Barbers must:**
- Recognize lesions
- **Refer abnormal conditions to a physician/dermatologist** before services

---

### Primary Skin Lesions

Different color, raised above surface:

| Lesion | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Bulla** | Large blister with watery fluid | Contact dermatitis, burns |
| **Cyst/Tubercle** | Closed sac with fluid/semisolid | Severe acne cyst, lipoma |
| **Macule** | Flat spot/discoloration | Freckle, liver spot |
| **Nodule** | Solid bump >0.4 inch | Swollen lymph nodes |
| **Papule** | Small elevation, no pus | Acne, warts |
| **Pustule** | Raised inflamed papule with pus | Acne, impetigo |
| **Tumor** | Abnormal mass | Cancer possible — medical referral |
| **Vesicle** | Small blister with clear fluid | Poison ivy/oak |
| **Wheal** | Itchy swollen lesion | Hives, insect bites |

---

### Secondary Skin Lesions

From accumulation or changes in primary lesions:

| Lesion | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Crust** | Dead cells/scab | Scab, sore |
| **Excoriation** | Skin sore/abrasion from scratching | Nail cuticle damage |
| **Fissure** | Crack penetrating dermis | Chapped hands/lips |
| **Keloid** | Thick scar from excessive fibrous growth | |
| **Scale** | Thin, dry, oily flakes | Dandruff, psoriasis |
| **Scar (Cicatrix)** | Mark after injury/healing | |
| **Ulcer** | Open lesion with loss of skin depth | Chicken pox, herpes, diabetes complications |

**Caution:** Never perform services on infectious/contagious conditions (e.g., pediculosis, scabies). Refer inflamed/irritated skin or unknown lesions to a physician.

### Discuss Disorders of the Sebaceous and Sudoriferous Glands (LO4 & LO5)

#### Sebaceous Gland Disorders:

| Disorder | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Comedones** | Blackheads (open) or whiteheads (closed) |
| **Milia** | Small, benign keratin-filled cysts (common in newborns/around eyes) |
| **Acne (Acne Vulgaris)** | Chronic inflammation; grades I–IV; caused by bacteria (Propionibacterium acnes) in clogged follicles |
| **Sebaceous Cyst** | Large pocket filled with sebum (may need surgical removal) |
| **Seborrhea / Seborrheic Dermatitis** | Oily, red, scaly, crusty (dandruff-like on scalp) |
| **Rosacea** | Chronic flushing, redness, dilated vessels, papules/pustules on cheeks/nose |
| **Asteatosis** | Dry, scaly skin from sebum deficiency |
| **Steatoma** | Sebaceous cyst or fatty tumor (wen) |

#### Sudoriferous Gland Disorders:

| Disorder | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Anhidrosis** | Deficiency or inability to sweat (life-threatening in severe cases) |
| **Bromhidrosis** | Foul-smelling sweat (often bacterial) |
| **Hyperhidrosis** | Excessive sweating |
| **Miliaria Rubra (Prickly Heat)** | Acute inflammation with small red vesicles and itching from heat |

### Common Inflammations and Infections (LO4)

| Condition | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Dermatitis** | General term for skin inflammation (irritant or allergic contact dermatitis) |
| **Eczema** | Chronic inflammatory condition with itching/burning |
| **Psoriasis** | Chronic red patches with silvery scales |
| **Herpes Simplex** | Recurring viral infection (cold sores/fever blisters) |

### Recognize Pigment Disorders and Hypertrophies (LO6 & LO7)

#### Pigment Disorders:

| Disorder | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Hyperpigmentation** | Darker than normal (chloasma/mask of pregnancy, lentigines/freckles) |
| **Hypopigmentation** | Lighter or white (leukoderma, vitiligo) |
| **Albinism** | Congenital absence of melanin (white hair, pink eyes/skin) |
| **Nevus (Mole)** | Small brownish spot; monitor changes |
| **Stain (Port-wine stain)** | Permanent discoloration |
| **Tan** | Temporary from sun/UV exposure |

#### Hypertrophies (abnormal growths):

| Growth | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Keratoma** | Thickened patch (callus or corn) |
| **Mole** | Small growth; some raised/dark with hair |
| **Skin Tag** | Small outgrowth (neck/chest) |
| **Verruca (Wart)** | Viral; contagious; can spread by scratching |

---

### Skin Cancer (LO8)

| Type | Description | Severity |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Basal Cell Carcinoma** | Most common, least severe; pearly nodules | High survival with early treatment |
| **Squamous Cell Carcinoma** | More serious; scaly red papules | Survival depends on stage |
| **Malignant Melanoma** | Least common but most dangerous (100% fatal if untreated); black/brown uneven patches | Early detection critical |

**ABCDE of Melanoma Detection:**
- **A**symmetry
- **B**order irregularity
- **C**olor variation
- **D**iameter (>6mm)
- **E**volution (changing)

**Did You Know?** Barbers are often first to notice skin/scalp changes — report growths or color changes to clients and suggest dermatologist visit.

### Know How to Maintain the Health of Your Skin

| Factor | Recommendation |
|--------|----------------|
| **Diet** | Balanced intake of fats, carbohydrates, proteins |
| **Vitamins/Supplements** | A, C, D, E support healing (consult physician) |
| **Water** | Maintains cell health and toxin elimination |
| **Protection** | Sunscreen (SPF 30+), moisturizers, avoid excessive sun/alcohol/tobacco |

**American Cancer Society Recommendation:** Regular self-exams and professional checkups for skin cancer.

### KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. **Skin** = largest organ; first line of defense
2. **Epidermis** = outermost, no blood vessels, 5 layers
3. **Dermis** = 25× thicker than epidermis; contains glands, vessels, follicles
4. **Melanin** = pigment from melanocytes; protects against UV
5. **Sebaceous glands** = oil/sebum; **Sudoriferous glands** = sweat
6. **SHAPES** = Skin functions (Sensation, Heat regulation, Absorption, Protection, Excretion, Secretion)
7. **Primary lesions** = original (bullae, papules, pustules); **Secondary lesions** = evolved (crusts, scales, scars)
8. **Acne** = grades I–IV; caused by Propionibacterium acnes
9. **Rosacea** = chronic flushing, redness, triggers include sun/spicy food/alcohol
10. **Three skin cancers:** Basal cell (most common), Squamous cell, Melanoma (most dangerous)
11. **ABCDE** = Melanoma detection
12. **Never treat** infectious/contagious conditions — **refer to physician**

### PRACTICE QUESTIONS

1. What are the two main divisions of the skin?
   → Epidermis and Dermis

2. What is the deepest layer of the epidermis and what does it contain?
   → Stratum germinativum (basal cell layer); contains melanocytes

3. What is melanin and what does it do?
   → Pigment produced by melanocytes; gives skin color and protects against UV damage

4. What are the two types of skin glands and their functions?
   → Sebaceous (oil/sebum for lubrication) and Sudoriferous (sweat for temperature regulation)

5. What does SHAPES stand for?
   → Sensation, Heat regulation, Absorption, Protection, Excretion, Secretion

6. What is the difference between primary and secondary skin lesions?
   → Primary = original lesions; Secondary = evolved from primary lesions

7. What causes acne?
   → Bacteria (Propionibacterium acnes) in clogged follicles

8. What are the ABCDE signs of melanoma?
   → Asymmetry, Border, Color, Diameter, Evolution

9. What should you do if you see a suspicious skin condition on a client?
   → Do not perform service; refer to physician/dermatologist

10. What is the most dangerous type of skin cancer?
    → Malignant melanoma
