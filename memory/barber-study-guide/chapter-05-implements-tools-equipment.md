# Chapter 5: Implements, Tools, and Equipment
## Study Guide Notes

---

### Introduction

The practice of barbering requires a variety of **implements, tools, and equipment**.

- Strive to purchase **high-quality items** that perform effectively and safely
- Avoid cheap "bargains" — low-quality tools bend, break, or wear out quickly and end up costing more to replace
- Well-made, properly maintained tools provide **years of dependable service**
- Ask your instructor or experienced barbers for guidance when selecting new items

---

### Why Study Implements, Tools, and Equipment?

Barbers should study these thoroughly because:
- Understanding purpose and capabilities helps **select the right tool for the job**
- Knowing parts helps follow instructions, comply with infection control, and recognize when repair/replacement is needed
- Proper holding and use is **critical for achieving desired results**
- Correct and safe use **protects you and your clients**

---

### Identify Different Types of Combs and Brushes (LO2)

#### Comb Basics

- Good combs are **slightly flexible** with smooth, rounded teeth that glide without snagging
- Most haircutting combs: **7–8 inches long** (straight, tapered, or handled)

#### Comb Materials:

| Material | Characteristics |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Hard rubber** | Flexible/durable but deteriorates in disinfectant |
| **Carbon** | Antistatic, heat/chemical resistant |
| **Graphite** | Less rigid |
| **Metal** | Retains heat but may corrode |

#### Comb Teeth & Styles:

| Comb Type | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| **Narrow/fine teeth** | Merge strands for precision cutting/detail |
| **Wide/coarse teeth** | Detangle, distribute product, handle large amounts of hair |
| **Combination combs** | Versatility without switching tools |
| **All-purpose/styling combs** | General cutting/styling |
| **Taper combs** | Blending, necklines, mustaches, ears |
| **Wide-tooth combs** | Detangling curly hair, applying relaxer |
| **Tail combs** | Sectioning, partings, wrapping, color |
| **Pick combs** | Textured or tightly curled hair |

#### Holding the Comb

- Practice proper vs. improper grips for **control and comfort**
- Shear-over-comb technique requires proper comb positioning

### Know About Haircutting Shears (LO3 & LO4)

One of the **most important tools** — proper selection and fit are vital.

#### Shear Styles

- **French style** — With finger rest/tang (preferred for balance/control)
- **German style** — No tang

#### Cast vs. Forged Shears

| Type | Characteristics |
|------|-----------------|
| **Cast** | Cheaper, more porous/weak, less sharp |
| **Forged** | Hammered/compressed; denser, more durable, stays sharp longer, higher quality |

#### Basic Parts (Figure 5-12)

- Moving blade + still blade
- Tension/pivot screw
- Points
- Cutting edges
- Shanks
- Bumper
- Finger grip
- Thumb grip
- Finger rest/tang

#### Blade Edges

| Edge Type | Characteristics |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Convex** | Very sharp, glides easily, clean cuts, less tension, quieter, good for slide cutting; sharpen every 4–6 months |
| **Beveled** | Serrated, grips hair, more tension, durable, stays sharp longer (9–12 months); **not for slide cutting** |

#### Other Features

- **Set** — Blade alignment
- **Tension screw** — Adjusts blade pressure
- **Shank** — Connects blade to handle
- **Size** — Most prefer 6½"–7½"

#### Handle Designs

| Design | Benefit |
|--------|---------|
| **Opposing** | Traditional |
| **Offset** | Ergonomic |
| **Crane** | Reduces stress on hand/wrist |

#### Texturizing, Thinning, and Blending Shears

| Type | Use |
|------|-----|
| **Texturizing/chunking** | Wider teeth for patterns/texture |
| **Thinning/blending** | Many narrow teeth to remove bulk without lines |

### Show How to Hold the Shears and Comb (LO5 & LO6)

Proper holding gives **maximum control and prevents strain**.

- **Ring finger** in finger grip (still blade)
- **Little finger** on brace
- **Thumb** in thumb grip (moving blade) — not past cuticle

#### Palming Techniques:

- **Palming the shears** — Pivot into palm while combing
- **Palming the comb** — Transfer after combing to free fingers

**Note:** Right-handed vs. left-handed shears have **reversed assembly**.

#### Maintaining Shears

- Clean/disinfect after each use
- Adjust tension daily (25–35% drop test)
- **Never:**
  - Drop them
  - Cut anything but human hair
  - Use harsh chemicals
- Protect in transport

### Know About Clippers and Outliners (LO7 & LO8)

Essential for **blending, texturizing, finishing, and detail**.

#### Two Main Clipper Types

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Detachable-blade** | Blades removed/attached while running |
| **Adjustable-blade** | Open/close with lever; guards extend length |

#### Parts

- Still blade
- Moving blade
- Heel
- On/off switch
- Blade adjustment lever

#### Motor Types

| Type | Characteristics |
|------|-----------------|
| **Rotary** | High power/speed, heavy-duty |
| **Pivot** | Quieter, good for thick/coarse hair |
| **Magnetic** | Reliable for fine textures |

#### Outliners/Trimmers

- Fine blades
- **Light pressure** to avoid irritation/ingrown hairs
- Straight or **T-shaped blade** (T-blade preferred for rounded areas)
- Essential for precision and facial hair

**Cordless Models:** Convenient/portable but may have less power.

---

### Describe Blades and Guards (LO9)

#### Blades

- Materials: **carbon steel** or **ceramic**
- Various sizes (**00000 = closest**)

#### Guards (Attachment Combs)

- Materials: plastic/rubber/steel
- Allow longer lengths than bare blades
- **Supplemental only** — not for infection control

#### Holding Clippers/Trimmers

- Technique varies by head section
- Use **free wrist movement** and **light hand** for outliners

**Caution:** Never adjust outliner blades flush; follow manufacturer guidelines.

---

### Know About Straight Razors (LO10 & LO11)

**Sharpest tool** for facial/head/neck shaves and finish work.

#### Two Types

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Changeable-blade (disposable blade)** | Preferred in shops for infection control and convenience |
| **Conventional** | Requires honing and stropping |

#### Parts

- Head, back, shoulder, tang, handle, point, edge, blade, heel, shank, pivot

#### Razor Shapers/Hair Razors

- Lightweight for **texturizing**

---

### Show How to Hold a Straight Razor (LO12–LO15)

Holding varies by service:

| Service | Grip |
|---------|------|
| **Shaving** | Ball of thumb + first two fingers on shank; little finger on tang |
| **Honing/stropping** | Flat sides of shank |
| **Haircutting** | Thumb at bottom or handle straightened |
| **Palming with comb** | Blade away from comb |

**State Regulatory Alert:** Some boards prohibit conventional straight razors — **check local rules**.

---

### Conventional Straight Razors

Requires **honing and stropping**.

#### Quality Factors

- Balance
- Temper
- Grind
- Finish
- Size
- Style

#### Grinds

| Grind | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Concave (hollow-ground)** | Preferred for sharpening and detecting resistance |
| **Wedge** | Sharp angle; excellent for coarse beards but challenging |

#### Finish, Size, Style, Care

- **Crocus finish** is premium
- Common sizes: ⅝", ⅞", etc.
- **Care:** Clean, strop, oil edge; avoid dropping

#### Accessories for Conventional Straight Razors

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Hone** | Grinds sharp edge (natural, synthetic, combination) |
| **Strop** | Smooths/polishes (combination, canvas, Russian, shell) |

#### Honing (Procedure 5-1)

- Detailed steps with even pressure
- Diagonal strokes
- Equal sides
- Gradual lightening
- Test on moistened thumbnail

#### Stropping (Procedure 5-2)

- Attach to chair
- Reverse direction from honing
- Roll on back
- Even diagonal strokes
- Test on thumb

### KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

1. **Avoid cheap tools** — they cost more to replace than quality tools
2. **Principal tools:** Combs, brushes, shears, clippers, outliners, razors
3. **Comb materials:** Hard rubber, carbon, graphite, metal
4. **Forged shears** are higher quality than cast shears
5. **Convex edges** are sharper but need more frequent sharpening; **beveled edges** last longer but can't slide cut
6. **Three handle designs:** Opposing, Offset, Crane
7. **Two clipper types:** Detachable-blade and Adjustable-blade
8. **Three motor types:** Rotary, Pivot, Magnetic
9. **00000 blade** = closest cut
10. **Changeable-blade razors** preferred for infection control; **conventional razors** require honing/stropping
11. **Some states prohibit conventional straight razors** — check local rules
12. **Modern clipper removal methods:** Towel, neck strips, vacuum (no traditional neck duster)

### PRACTICE QUESTIONS

1. What is the difference between cast and forged shears?
   → Cast: cheaper, porous, less sharp; Forged: denser, more durable, stays sharp longer

2. What are the two main types of clipper blades?
   → Detachable-blade and Adjustable-blade

3. What are the three motor types in clippers?
   → Rotary, Pivot, Magnetic

4. Which blade edge is better for slide cutting?
   → Convex edge

5. Why are changeable-blade razors preferred in shops?
   → Better infection control and convenience

6. What is the purpose of a strop?
   → Smooths and polishes the razor edge after honing

7. What are modern safe methods for removing hair clippings?
   → Towel, paper neck strips, vacuum systems

8. What size shear do most barbers prefer?
   → 6½"–7½"
