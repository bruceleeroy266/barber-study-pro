CHAPTER 6

General Anatomy & Physiology

Cells, tissues, organs, and the 11 body systems relevant to barbering

📋 Introduction

Understanding anatomy and physiology is essential for barbers to:

  • Perform services safely and effectively
  • Recognize normal versus abnormal conditions
  • Avoid causing injury or discomfort to clients
  • Make informed decisions about when to refer a client to a physician
Anatomy = the study of body structure (what it's made of)
Physiology = the study of body function (how it works)

🔬 Cells — The Basic Unit of Life

All living organisms are composed of cells. A typical cell contains:

  • Protoplasm — Colorless, jelly-like substance containing proteins, fats, carbohydrates, mineral salts, and water
  • Nucleus — Control center; directs cell reproduction and contains DNA
  • Cytoplasm — Fluid surrounding the nucleus containing organelles
  • Cell membrane — Outer layer that encloses the cell and regulates what enters/exits

Cells reproduce by mitosis (cell division), allowing tissues to grow and repair.

🧠 Nervous System

The nervous system controls and coordinates all body functions. Divided into:

  • Central Nervous System (CNS) — Brain and spinal cord
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) — Nerves connecting CNS to rest of body

Cranial Nerves (Most Relevant for Barbers)

There are 12 pairs of cranial nerves. The most relevant for barbering:

NerveRoman NumeralFunctionBarbering Relevance
TrigeminalVSensation to face, scalp, teeth; chewing musclesFacial sensation, shaving
FacialVIIControls facial expressions, taste (front tongue)Facial massage, shaving comfort
Important: The trigeminal nerve (V) is the largest cranial nerve and provides sensation to most of the face and scalp. Understanding its pathways helps avoid causing discomfort during services.

❤️ Circulatory System

The circulatory system transports blood, oxygen, nutrients, and waste throughout the body.

  • Heart — Pumps blood through the system
  • Arteries — Carry blood away from the heart
  • Capillaries — Tiny vessels where exchange occurs
  • Veins — Carry blood back to the heart

Principal Veins of the Head, Face, and Neck

Blood returns to the heart via two principal veins on each side of the neck:

VeinLocationFunction
Internal jugular veinSide of the neckCollects blood from the brain and parts of the face and neck
External jugular veinSide of the neckCarries blood returning to the heart from the head, face, and neck
Important: The most important veins of the face and neck parallel the arteries and take the same names.

🛡️ Lymphatic/Immune System

The lymphatic/immune system is made up of:

  • Lymph
  • Lymph nodes
  • Thymus gland
  • Spleen
  • Lymph vessels

Function

Carries waste and impurities away from the cells and protects the body from disease by:

  • Developing immunity
  • Destroying disease-causing microorganisms

Lymph

  • Clear fluid that circulates in the lymph spaces (lymphatics) of the body
  • Helps carry wastes and impurities away from the cells before returning to the circulatory system

Connection to Cardiovascular System

  • The lymphatic system drains tissue spaces of excess interstitial fluid
  • Closely connected to the cardiovascular system
  • Both transport fluids; lymphatic system transports lymph, which eventually returns to the blood

Primary Functions:

  1. Carry nourishment from the blood to the body cells
  2. Act as a defense against toxins and bacteria
  3. Remove by-products of infection such as pus and dead tissue
  4. Remove waste material from the body cells to the blood
  5. Provide a suitable fluid environment for the cells

🧴 Integumentary System

The integumentary system consists of:

  • Skin
  • Accessory organs (oil and sweat glands, sensory receptors, hair, and nails)

Function: Protective covering and helps regulate the body's temperature.

Integument = "a natural covering"

Skin as Protection

Think of skin as a protective overcoat against:

  • Germs
  • Chemicals
  • Sun exposure
Skin is water resistant.

🔮 Endocrine System

The endocrine system is a group of specialized glands that affect:

  • Growth
  • Development
  • Sexual functions
  • Health of the entire body

Glands

Glands are secretory organs that remove and release certain elements from the blood to convert them into new compounds.

Two Main Types of Glands:

TypeDescriptionExamples
Endocrine glands (ductless)Release hormonal secretions directly into bloodstreamThyroid, pituitary glands
Exocrine glands (duct)Produce substance that travels through small tubelike ductsSweat and oil glands of skin

Hormones

  • Secretions (insulin, adrenaline, estrogen) that stimulate functional activity or other secretions in the body
  • Influence the welfare of the entire body

🧬 Reproductive System

Female:

  • Ovaries
  • Uterine tubes
  • Uterus
  • Vagina

Male:

  • Testes
  • Prostate gland
  • Penis
  • Urethra

Function

Produces offspring and passes on the genetic code from one generation to another.

Hormones and Skin/Hair

The reproductive system produces hormones:

  • Females: Primarily estrogen
  • Males: Primarily testosterone

These hormones affect and change the skin:

  • Acne
  • Loss of scalp hair
  • Facial hair growth and color
  • Darker skin pigmentations
Barbering relevance: Hormonal changes (puberty, aging, medical conditions) directly affect the services you provide — hair growth patterns, skin conditions, and hair texture changes.

🫁 Respiratory & Excretory Systems

Respiratory System

  • Lungs and air passages
  • Function: Brings oxygen into the body and removes carbon dioxide
  • Barbering relevance: Be aware of client breathing during facial services; some products/fragrances may affect sensitive individuals

Excretory System

  • Skin (sweat glands), kidneys, lungs, liver
  • Function: Removes waste products from the body
  • Barbering relevance: Sweat glands are part of both excretory and integumentary systems; understanding perspiration helps with product recommendations

🍽️ Digestive System

  • Mouth, esophagus, stomach, intestines, liver, pancreas
  • Function: Breaks down food into nutrients the body can use
  • Barbering relevance (indirect):
    • Nutrition affects hair and skin health
    • Protein, vitamins, and minerals from diet support hair growth
    • Poor nutrition can lead to dry, brittle hair and skin problems
